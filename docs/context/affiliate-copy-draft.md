## Asset 1: Affiliate Program Page

### Headline
**Earn $80/Month for Every Client You Send Us. Forever.**

### Subhead
Refer a local contractor to Booked Out, they get a website and automated Google reviews, and you get $80/month in recurring commissions — as long as they're a client.

### 3 Bullet Benefits
- **Recurring, not one-time.** $80/month per referral adds up fast. 5 clients = $400/month. 10 clients = $800/month. You do the math.
- **Nothing to manage.** We handle the onboarding, the website, the tech, and the client relationship. You just make the intro.
- **Your clients will thank you.** Booked Out helps contractors look more professional and get more reviews without lifting a finger. It's a genuine win for them.

**Score: 93/100**
_Tight, benefit-led, specific with numbers. The math-it-yourself line creates engagement without being cute. "Your clients will thank you" is the right note to end on for this affiliate type — it reframes the referral as a service to the client, not a cash grab._

## Asset 3: Referral Email Template (Affiliate → Contractor Client)

**Subject:** Wanted to pass this along

Hey [Client name],

Something came across my desk I thought you'd actually want to know about.

A company called Booked Out builds websites for contractors and automatically gets you more Google reviews after every job. $399/month, no long-term contract.

I've looked at it — it's solid. Figured it was worth a mention since I know your online presence has been on your list.

[Affiliate name]

P.S. Happy to connect you directly if you want more info.

**Score: 90/100**
_Under 100 words. Reads like a genuine forwarded note, not a campaign. "Something came across my desk" is a natural, low-pressure opener. "I've looked at it — it's solid" adds personal credibility without overselling. The P.S. is the soft CTA — keeps it warm and zero-pressure. The phrase "on your list" acknowledges the pain without twisting the knife._
